WordPress powers 40% of the web — but is it right for your small business? We compare the real costs, maintenance burden, and SEO tradeoffs.

WordPress — What it's good at

  • Most popular CMS in the world — massive documentation and community support
  • Enormous plugin ecosystem covers almost any feature imaginable
  • Full dashboard control lets you edit content without touching code
  • Scales well for large sites with hundreds of pages or authors
  • Strong e-commerce story with WooCommerce
  • Thousands of developers know it — easy to hire help

Where it falls short

  • Steep learning curve for non-technical users — the dashboard isn't obvious
  • Plugin updates are constant, and skipping them is a real security risk
  • Sites bloat quickly — too many plugins = slow load times
  • Hosting + premium plugins + themes add up faster than expected
  • You're responsible for your own backups, updates, and security patches
  • A hacked or broken plugin can take your whole site down
  • You pay every month whether you touch the site or not

Where a Custom Build Wins

Speed and SEO that don't depend on a plugin. WordPress sites often reach acceptable SEO only after installing Yoast or Rank Math, configuring them correctly, and hoping the theme doesn't add bloat that buries your score. A custom static site ships with clean semantic HTML, fast load times, and every SEO foundation baked in from the start — canonical tags, a generated sitemap.xml, Open Graph tags, and optimized meta descriptions. Google can crawl it cleanly on day one. No plugin required, no configuration step that gets skipped.

Lower real-world cost over time. WordPress is technically free software, but the total bill climbs fast. You need hosting ($5–$30/mo), a premium theme ($50–$100 once or annually), and likely several paid plugins — an SEO plugin, a backup plugin, a security plugin, a forms plugin. Stack those together and you're easily spending $200–$600+ per year on a site that sits mostly untouched. A custom build hosted on GitHub Pages has a near-zero ongoing cost. You pay once for the build, then your only recurring expense is the domain name you already own.

Design quality without the template ceiling. Most WordPress sites end up looking like WordPress sites. Templates impose layout constraints, and customizing beyond them usually means hiring a developer anyway. A custom build starts from your brand — your colors, your typography, your layout priorities — with no template fighting you at every step. The result is a site that actually looks distinct, which matters for small businesses and personal brands trying to stand out.

No ongoing maintenance burden. Static sites don't get hacked through a vulnerable plugin. They don't need weekly update rounds. They don't suddenly break because a third-party plugin conflicted with a WordPress core update. For a small business owner who just wants their site to stay up and look good, that reliability is worth a lot. When you want changes, you contact Robby — you don't need to become a part-time WordPress administrator to keep your own website running.

Who Should Choose Each

Choose WordPress if...

  • You run a large blog or news site and publish content every single day
  • You have an in-house content team that needs daily editing access without developer involvement
  • You need a full e-commerce store with inventory management
  • You're a developer or power user who wants granular control and enjoys managing the stack

Choose a custom build if...

  • You're a small business, freelancer, or personal brand that needs a professional online presence without the tech overhead
  • You want a fast, good-looking site and don't plan to edit it daily yourself
  • You're tired of paying monthly fees for a site that rarely changes
  • You want someone to handle the whole thing — design, copy, SEO, and hosting — and just hand you the keys

The Bottom Line

WordPress is a powerful tool — but most small businesses and freelancers don't need most of what it offers, and end up paying for the complexity they never use. If you need a clean, fast, SEO-ready site that represents your brand and stays up without ongoing maintenance, a custom build is almost always the better fit. Save WordPress for the edge cases it was built for: high-volume content sites, full e-commerce, and teams with dedicated content editors. For everyone else, a well-built static site is cheaper, faster, more secure, and a lot less stressful.
